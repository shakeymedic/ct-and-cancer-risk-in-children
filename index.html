Clinical Bottom Line

"Justify every scan. If Ultrasound or MRI can answer the question, use them. However, never withhold a clinically indicated CT scan based on this risk alone—the immediate risk of a missed diagnosis usually outweighs the long-term cancer risk."

Source: NEJM 2025

Potential FRCEM OSCE Questions

"What is the main limitation of this study being retrospective?"

Key Answer: Reverse Causation / Indication Bias.

Children who get scans are inherently sicker than those who don't. While the authors adjusted for Down's syndrome and used a lag period (ignoring cancers diagnosed within 6-24 months of a scan), it is impossible to completely rule out that the scan was performed to investigate symptoms of an as-yet-undiagnosed cancer.

"A parent is refusing a Head CT for their child with a GCS of 13 post-trauma due to cancer risk. How do you counsel them?"

Key Answer: Acknowledge, Validate, then Balance.

1. Validate: "It is excellent that you are thinking about long-term safety."
2. Quantify Risk: "The risk is real but very small. For every 10,000 scans, we might see ~25 extra cases over 20 years."
3. Immediate Benefit: "However, the risk of missing a bleed in the brain right now is much higher and could be fatal tonight."

"Does this paper mean we should stop using CTs in children?"

Key Answer: No.

It means we should apply ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) principles strictly. We should not use CT if an MRI or Ultrasound can answer the question (e.g., abdominal pain). But for time-critical, life-threatening queries (polytrauma, intracranial haemorrhage), CT remains the gold standard and its benefits outweigh the risks.
